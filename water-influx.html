13.3 Water Influx Models

13.3.1 The Pot Aquifer Model

The Pot Aquifer Model is the simplest representation of water influx. It assumes that the aquifer acts like a storage tank (or pot) and provides water influx based solely on the pressure difference between the aquifer and the reservoir. This model is widely used for reservoirs with limited water drive or where the aquifer size is relatively small compared to the reservoir.

13.3.1.1 Assumptions of the Pot Aquifer Model

  1. The aquifer behaves as a single unit with uniform pressure.
  2. Water influx is proportional to the pressure difference between the aquifer and the reservoir.
  3. No time delay is considered for water flow within the aquifer.
  4. Compressibility of water and pore volume are constant.

13.3.1.2 Governing Equation

The cumulative water influx (\(W_e\)) is proportional to the pressure drop in the reservoir: \[ W_e = c_w \cdot V_a \cdot (p_a - p) \] Where: - \(W_e\): Cumulative water influx (bbl) - \(c_w\): Water and pore compressibility (1/psi) - \(V_a\): Aquifer pore volume (bbl) - \(p_a\): Initial aquifer pressure (psi) - \(p\): Current reservoir pressure (psi)

13.3.1.3 Derivation of the Governing Equation

  1. Volume Change:
    The volume of water entering the reservoir is due to the compression of water and the expansion of pore space: \[ \Delta V = c_w \cdot V_a \cdot \Delta p \] Where:
    • \(\Delta V\): Volume of water influx (bbl)
    • \(\Delta p = p_a - p\): Pressure drop (psi)
  2. Cumulative Water Influx:
    Integrating the volume change over time gives the cumulative water influx: \[ W_e = c_w \cdot V_a \cdot (p_a - p) \]

13.3.1.4 Practical Applications

  1. Small Aquifers:
    This model is best suited for reservoirs with small or limited aquifers where pressure gradients within the aquifer can be neglected.

  2. Initial Estimates:
    The Pot Aquifer Model is often used for quick initial estimates of water influx before switching to more complex models.

# Example Python Code: Pot Aquifer Model
def pot_aquifer_water_influx(c_w, V_a, p_a, p):
    """
    Calculate cumulative water influx using the Pot Aquifer Model.

    Parameters:
        c_w (float): Water and pore compressibility (1/psi).
        V_a (float): Aquifer pore volume (bbl).
        p_a (float): Initial aquifer pressure (psi).
        p (float): Current reservoir pressure (psi).

    Returns:
        float: Cumulative water influx (bbl).
    """
    return c_w * V_a * (p_a - p)

# Given parameters
c_w = 3e-6  # 1/psi
V_a = 2e7  # bbl
p_a = 3000  # psi
p = 2500  # psi

# Calculate cumulative water influx
W_e = pot_aquifer_water_influx(c_w, V_a, p_a, p)
print(f"Cumulative Water Influx (W_e): {W_e:.2f} bbl")

13.3.2 Schilthuis Steady-State Model

The Schilthuis Steady-State Model assumes that water influx occurs under steady-state flow conditions, where the flow rate is constant, and the pressure gradient across the aquifer does not change with time. This model is widely used in cases where the aquifer quickly responds to pressure changes in the reservoir, maintaining steady flow.

13.3.2.1 Assumptions of the Schilthuis Steady-State Model

  1. The aquifer behaves as an infinite system with no boundary effects.
  2. Steady-state flow conditions exist, meaning the pressure gradient across the aquifer remains constant.
  3. Water influx is directly proportional to the pressure difference between the aquifer and the reservoir.

13.3.2.2 Governing Equation

The rate of water influx (\(W_e\)) is given by: \[ W_e = J \cdot (p_a - p) \] Where: - \(W_e\): Water influx rate (bbl/day) - \(J\): Productivity index of the aquifer (bbl/day/psi) - \(p_a\): Average aquifer pressure (psi) - \(p\): Reservoir pressure (psi)

The cumulative water influx over time is: \[ W_e(t) = J \cdot \int_0^t (p_a - p) \, dt \]

13.3.2.3 Derivation of the Steady-State Flow Equation

  1. Darcyâ€™s Law:
    For radial flow in a porous medium: \[ q = \frac{2 \pi k h (p_a - p)}{\mu \ln(r_e / r_w)} \]

  2. Aquifer Productivity Index (\(J\)):
    Define the aquifer productivity index as: \[ J = \frac{2 \pi k h}{\mu \ln(r_e / r_w)} \] Substituting this into Darcyâ€™s law: \[ W_e = J \cdot (p_a - p) \]

  3. Cumulative Water Influx:
    To find the cumulative water influx over time, integrate the rate of water influx: \[ W_e(t) = \int_0^t W_e \, dt = J \cdot \int_0^t (p_a - p) \, dt \]

13.3.2.4 Applications of the Schilthuis Model

  1. Aquifers with Fast Response:
    Suitable for aquifers where steady-state flow conditions are quickly established.

  2. Pressure Maintenance:
    Used to estimate the water influx required to maintain reservoir pressure during production.

13.3.3 Example Calculation

Suppose the following parameters: - \(J = 100\) bbl/day/psi - \(p_a = 3000\) psi - \(p = 2500\) psi - Time of interest: \(t = 10\) days

The water influx rate is: \[ W_e = J \cdot (p_a - p) = 100 \cdot (3000 - 2500) = 50000 \, \text{bbl/day} \]

Cumulative water influx over 10 days is: \[ W_e(t) = W_e \cdot t = 50000 \cdot 10 = 500000 \, \text{bbl} \]

13.3.4 Python Code: Schilthuis Steady-State Model

def schilthuis_water_influx_rate(J, p_a, p):
    """
    Calculate the water influx rate using the Schilthuis Steady-State Model.

    Parameters:
        J (float): Productivity index of the aquifer (bbl/day/psi).
        p_a (float): Average aquifer pressure (psi).
        p (float): Reservoir pressure (psi).

    Returns:
        float: Water influx rate (bbl/day).
    """
    return J * (p_a - p)

def cumulative_water_influx(W_e, time):
    """
    Calculate the cumulative water influx over a given time period.

    Parameters:
        W_e (float): Water influx rate (bbl/day).
        time (float): Time period (days).

    Returns:
        float: Cumulative water influx (bbl).
    """
    return W_e * time

p_a = 3000  # psi
p = 2500  # psi

def calculate_water_influx(c, pressure_data, t_intervals, initial_pressure):
    """
    Calculate cumulative water influx using the Schilthuis steady-state model.

    Parameters:
        c (float): Water influx constant (bbl/day/psi).
        pressure_data (list): Reservoir pressures at different times (psi).
        t_intervals (list): Time intervals corresponding to pressures (days).
        initial_pressure (float): Initial reservoir pressure (psi).

    Returns:
        list: Cumulative water influx values at each time step (bbl).
    """
    cumulative_influx = []
    previous_influx = 0
    delta_pressure = []
    delta_time = []
    for i in range(len(t_intervals)):
        
        delta_p = (initial_pressure - pressure_data[i])
        delta_t = t_intervals[i] - (t_intervals[i - 1] if i > 0 else 0)
        delta_pressure.append(delta_p)
        delta_time.append(delta_t)
        
        for j in range(len(delta_pressure)-2, len(delta_pressure)):
            water_influx = c * delta_pressure[j] * delta_time[j]/2
            previous_influx += water_influx
            
        cumulative_influx.append(previous_influx)
    return cumulative_influx

# Calculate cumulative water influx

# Display results
for t, influx in zip(t_intervals, cumulative_influx):
    print("_"*50)

13.3.5 Hurst Modified Steady-State Model

The Hurst Modified Steady-State Model refines the Schilthuis steady-state model by considering the time-dependent behavior of finite aquifers. This model accounts for the fact that steady-state conditions are not immediately established and that aquifers exhibit transient behavior before reaching equilibrium.

13.3.5.1 Assumptions of the Hurst Modified Model

  1. The aquifer is finite and bounded, resulting in time-dependent water influx.
  2. The pressure gradient across the aquifer changes with time until steady-state flow is established.
  3. The aquifer eventually reaches steady-state conditions if sufficient time is allowed.

13.3.5.2 Governing Equation

The cumulative water influx (\(W_e(t)\)) is expressed as: \[ W_e(t) = W_e^* \cdot \left(1 - e^{-\beta t}\right) \] Where: - \(W_e(t)\): Cumulative water influx at time \(t\) (bbl) - \(W_e^*\): Maximum (steady-state) water influx (bbl) - \(\beta\): Time constant (1/day) - \(t\): Time (days)

13.3.5.3 Derivation of the Governing Equation

  1. Steady-State Maximum Water Influx:
    Under steady-state conditions, the water influx rate is constant and reaches a maximum value: \[ W_e^* = J \cdot (p_a - p) \] Where \(J\) is the aquifer productivity index.

  2. Time-Dependent Behavior:
    For a finite aquifer, the water influx rate increases over time as the pressure gradient propagates through the aquifer. This time dependency is modeled as: \[ W_e(t) = W_e^* \cdot \left(1 - e^{-\beta t}\right) \] The exponential term \(\left(1 - e^{-\beta t}\right)\) accounts for the delayed establishment of steady-state conditions.

  3. Time Constant (\(\beta\)):
    The time constant is defined as: \[ \beta = \frac{k}{\phi \mu c \cdot V_a} \] Where:

    • \(k\): Permeability (md)
    • \(\phi\): Porosity (fraction)
    • \(\mu\): Fluid viscosity (cp)
    • \(c\): Total compressibility (1/psi)
    • \(V_a\): Aquifer pore volume (bbl)

13.3.5.4 Applications of the Hurst Modified Model

  1. Finite Aquifers:
    This model is ideal for reservoirs with finite aquifers where boundary effects significantly influence water influx.

  2. Transient Behavior:
    Used to predict water influx during the early stages of production before steady-state conditions are established.

13.3.6 Example Calculation

Suppose the following parameters: - \(W_e^* = 100000\) bbl - \(\beta = 0.005\) 1/day - Time: \(t = [0, 50, 100, 150, 200]\) days

The cumulative water influx at each time step can be calculated using: \[ W_e(t) = W_e^* \cdot \left(1 - e^{-\beta t}\right) \]

13.3.7 Python Code: Hurst Modified Steady-State Model

import math

def hurst_modified_water_influx(W_e_star, beta, times):
    """
    Calculate cumulative water influx using the Hurst Modified Steady-State Equation.

    Parameters:
        W_e_star (float): Maximum steady-state water influx (bbl).
        beta (float): Time constant (1/day).
        times (list): List of time points (days).

    Returns:
        list: Cumulative water influx at each time point (bbl).
    """
    return [W_e_star * (1 - math.exp(-beta * t)) for t in times]

# Given parameters
W_e_star = 100000  # bbl
beta = 0.005  # 1/day
times = [0, 50, 100, 150, 200]  # days

# Calculate cumulative water influx

# Display results
for t, W_e in zip(times, water_influx):
    print(f"Day {t}: Cumulative Water Influx = {W_e:.2f} bbl")

        delta_pressure (list): Pressure drop values (psi) corresponding to time intervals.

13.3.8 van Everdingen and Hurst Unsteady-State Model

The van Everdingen and Hurst Unsteady-State Model is one of the most widely used water influx models in reservoir engineering. It accounts for the unsteady-state behavior of aquifers by solving the diffusivity equation for transient flow in a radial system. This model is particularly useful for analyzing water influx from finite and infinite aquifers.

13.3.8.1 Assumptions of the Model

  1. The aquifer is homogeneous and isotropic.
  2. The aquifer is initially at uniform pressure.
  3. Water influx occurs due to a pressure difference between the aquifer and the reservoir.
  4. The reservoir pressure changes with time, resulting in transient flow conditions.

13.3.8.2 Governing Equation

The governing equation for radial flow in porous media under unsteady-state conditions is: \[ \frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = \eta \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial}{\partial r} \left( r \frac{\partial p}{\partial r} \right) \] Where: - \(p\): Pressure (psi) - \(t\): Time (days) - \(r\): Radial distance (ft) - \(\eta = \frac{k}{\phi \mu c}\): Diffusivity constant

The solution to this equation for water influx is expressed in terms of dimensionless parameters.

13.3.8.3 Dimensionless Parameters

  1. Dimensionless Pressure: \[ p_D = \frac{p_a - p}{p_a - p_w} \] Where:
    • \(p_a\): Average aquifer pressure (psi)
    • \(p_w\): Wellbore pressure (psi)
  2. Dimensionless Time: \[ t_D = \frac{\eta t}{r_e^2} \] Where:
    • \(r_e\): External radius of the aquifer (ft)
  3. Dimensionless Radius: \[ r_D = \frac{r}{r_e} \]

13.3.8.4 Water Influx Equation

The cumulative water influx (\(W_e\)) is given as: \[ W_e = \int_0^t J \cdot (p_a - p) \, dt \] Where: - \(J = \frac{2 \pi k h}{\mu \ln(r_e / r_w)}\): Aquifer productivity index (bbl/day/psi)

By introducing the dimensionless parameters and solving the diffusivity equation, the water influx becomes: \[ W_e = \frac{2 \pi k h (p_a - p_w)}{\mu} \cdot r_e^2 \cdot W_D \] Where \(W_D\) is the dimensionless water influx, a function of \(t_D\).

13.3.8.5 Infinite Aquifer Solution

For an infinite aquifer, the dimensionless water influx is obtained from the exponential integral: \[ W_D(t_D) = 1 - e^{-t_D} \]

The water influx for an infinite aquifer becomes: \[ W_e = \frac{2 \pi k h (p_a - p_w)}{\mu} \cdot r_e^2 \cdot (1 - e^{-t_D}) \]

13.3.8.6 Finite Aquifer Solution

For a finite aquifer, the boundary effects modify the solution. The dimensionless water influx \(W_D(t_D)\) is determined from tabulated solutions or numerical integration.

13.3.11 Carter and Tracy Water Influx Model

The Carter and Tracy Water Influx Model provides a simplified method to estimate water influx by using superposition principles in time. This model is particularly useful when dealing with reservoirs that experience variable pressure changes over time, as it avoids the complexity of solving the diffusivity equation directly.

13.3.11.1 Assumptions of the Model

  1. The aquifer is infinite and homogeneous.
  2. Water influx is proportional to the pressure difference between the aquifer and the reservoir.
  3. Superposition in time applies, allowing for cumulative pressure changes to be summed over discrete time intervals.

13.3.11.2 Governing Equation

The cumulative water influx (\(W_e\)) is given by: \[ W_e = C \cdot \sum_{i=1}^n \Delta p_i \cdot \Delta W_D(t_i - t_{i-1}) \] Where: - \(W_e\): Cumulative water influx (bbl) - \(C\): Aquifer constant, defined as: \[ C = \frac{2 \pi k h r_e^2}{\mu \ln(r_e / r_w)} \] - \(\Delta p_i = p_i - p_{i-1}\): Pressure change between successive time steps (psi) - \(\Delta W_D(t_i - t_{i-1})\): Dimensionless water influx increment over time interval \(t_i - t_{i-1}\)

13.3.11.3 Key Features

  1. Superposition Principle:
    The model sums the contributions of water influx over successive time intervals, making it ideal for variable pressure histories.

  2. Dimensionless Water Influx (\(W_D\)):
    The dimensionless water influx is tabulated or calculated based on: \[ W_D = 1 - e^{-t_D} \] Where: \[ t_D = \frac{\eta t}{r_e^2} \]

13.3.13 Python Code: Carter and Tracy Water Influx Model

import math

def carter_tracy_water_influx(k, h, mu, r_e, r_w, pressure_history, time_intervals):
    """
    Calculate cumulative water influx using the Carter and Tracy Water Influx Model.

    Parameters:
        k (float): Permeability (md).
        h (float): Aquifer thickness (ft).
        mu (float): Fluid viscosity (cp).
        r_e (float): External radius of the aquifer (ft).
        r_w (float): Wellbore radius (ft).
        pressure_history (list): Reservoir pressures at different times (psi).
        time_intervals (list): Time intervals corresponding to pressures (days).

    Returns:
        list: Cumulative water influx at each time step (bbl).
    """
    C = (2 * math.pi * k * h * r_e**2) / (mu * math.log(r_e / r_w))  # Aquifer constant
    W_e_list = []
    cumulative_influx = 0

    for i in range(1, len(time_intervals)):
        delta_p = pressure_history[i - 1] - pressure_history[i]
        delta_t = time_intervals[i] - time_intervals[i - 1]
        t_D = delta_t / r_e**2
        W_D_increment = 1 - math.exp(-t_D)  # Dimensionless water influx increment
        delta_W_e = C * delta_p * W_D_increment
        cumulative_influx += delta_W_e
        W_e_list.append(cumulative_influx)

    return W_e_list
